Parent Water Safety Tips

Age-appropriate guidance to keep your children safe

üë∂ Age-by-Age Guidelines

Infants (0-12 months)

  • Constant supervision: Within arm's reach at all times
  • Bath time: Never leave alone, even for a second - drowning can occur in just 1-2 inches of water
  • Swim lessons: Can start as early as 6 months with survival swim training
  • Survival skills focus: Floating, breath control, self-rescue
  • Around water: Always hold infant when near any body of water

Toddlers (1-3 years)

  • Highest risk age group - drowning is the leading cause of death
  • Constant supervision: Touch supervision (within arm's reach)
  • Swim lessons: Enroll in age-appropriate survival swim lessons
  • Barriers: Install 4-sided pool fence with self-latching gate
  • Teach water respect: Never go near water without permission
  • Life jackets: Must be worn around open water

Preschoolers (3-5 years)

  • Supervision: Close supervision - visual contact at all times
  • Swim lessons: Continue formal lessons, focus on fundamental skills
  • Water rules: Reinforce rules about asking permission before swimming
  • Life jackets: Use Coast Guard-approved life jackets for non-swimmers
  • No floaties: Water wings and inflatable toys are NOT safety devices

School-Age (6-12 years)

  • Supervision: Active supervision - know where they are and what they're doing
  • Swimming skills: Ensure proficiency - can they swim 25 yards and tread water for 60 seconds?
  • Buddy system: Never swim alone
  • Open water: Teach about currents, undertows, and changing conditions
  • Diving safety: Feet first, first time - always check water depth

Teens (13+ years)

  • Never swim alone - even strong swimmers
  • No alcohol: Alcohol impairs judgment and swimming ability
  • Lifeguard awareness: Swim only in supervised areas
  • Emergency skills: Learn CPR and rescue techniques
  • Role modeling: Set good examples for younger siblings

The Water Watcher System

Assign a responsible adult as the designated "Water Watcher"

  • No distractions - put away phones, books, and other activities
  • Rotate the responsibility every 15 minutes to maintain focus
  • Use a water watcher tag or card to pass between adults
  • The Water Watcher's ONLY job is watching the water

üèä What to Look for in Swim Lessons

  • Qualified instructors: Red Cross, YMCA, or other nationally recognized certification
  • Small class sizes: Maximum 4-6 students per instructor
  • Survival skills focus: Floating, breath control, self-rescue techniques
  • Age-appropriate curriculum: Tailored to developmental stage
  • Progress tracking: Clear skill milestones and assessments
  • Parent communication: Regular updates and practice guidance
  • Safe environment: Clean pool, proper equipment, emergency plan

  • Layers of protection: Supervision + Barriers + Lessons + Emergency Skills
  • Drowning is silent: There's no splashing or calling for help

  • Learn CPR: You could save your child's life
  • Check the pool first: If a child is missing, always check water first
